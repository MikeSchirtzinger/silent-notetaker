Checkpoint one. Chaos theory begins with a deceptively simple question about prediction. Checkpoint two. If we know the present state of a system exactly, classical mechanics promises we can compute its entire future. Checkpoint three. The catch is that we never know the present state exactly, and some systems amplify tiny errors at an exponential rate. Checkpoint four. The difference becomes especially clear in the standard double pendulum example. Checkpoint five. Two pendulums released from angles that differ by one thousandth of a degree will diverge completely within about ten swings. Checkpoint six. This sensitivity to initial conditions is measured by the Lyapunov exponent. Checkpoint seven. A positive Lyapunov exponent means nearby trajectories separate exponentially fast, doubling their distance over a characteristic time. Checkpoint eight. For the double pendulum that doubling time is roughly half a second, which is why prediction beyond a few seconds is hopeless. Checkpoint nine. Edward Lorenz discovered the same behavior in a twelve variable weather model in 1961. Checkpoint ten. He rounded a number from zero point five zero six one two seven to zero point five zero six and the simulated weather changed completely within two months. Checkpoint eleven. This is the origin of the butterfly effect, the idea that a small perturbation can redirect the large scale outcome. Checkpoint twelve. Importantly, chaos is not randomness, because the underlying equations remain fully deterministic. Checkpoint thirteen. The Lorenz attractor traces a butterfly shaped figure that never repeats and never intersects itself. Checkpoint fourteen. Its fractal dimension is about two point zero six, just slightly more than a flat surface. Checkpoint fifteen. Strange attractors like this one confine trajectories to a bounded region while keeping them locally unstable. Checkpoint sixteen. Weather forecasts lose most of their skill after about ten days for exactly this reason. Checkpoint seventeen. Ensemble forecasting attacks the problem by running fifty or more simulations from slightly perturbed initial states. Checkpoint eighteen. When the ensemble members agree, confidence is high, and when they spread apart, the atmosphere is in a chaotic regime. Checkpoint nineteen. The three body problem in celestial mechanics shows the same character, as Poincare proved in 1890. Checkpoint twenty. There is no closed form solution, and planetary orbits can be chaotic over hundreds of millions of years. Checkpoint twenty one. The inner solar system has a Lyapunov time of roughly five million years. Checkpoint twenty two. That means a one kilometer error in Earth's position today makes its orbital phase unpredictable beyond about one hundred million years. Checkpoint twenty three. Chaos also appears in fluid turbulence, population dynamics, cardiac arrhythmia, and even dripping faucets. Checkpoint twenty four. The logistic map shows how a one line equation can cascade from stability through period doubling into full chaos. Checkpoint twenty five. Feigenbaum found that the ratio of successive period doubling intervals converges to four point six six nine, a universal constant. Checkpoint twenty six. The same constant appears in completely unrelated systems, which hints at deep structure beneath the apparent disorder. Checkpoint twenty seven. So chaos gives us a humbling boundary on prediction, but also a new kind of order to study. Checkpoint twenty eight. The lesson is not that forecasting is futile, but that uncertainty itself can be quantified and managed.
